One of the first questions I get from first-time buyers is: "Should I go FHA or conventional?"

The honest answer is: it depends. And I'm going to walk you through exactly what it depends on so you can make the decision that's right for your situation.

## The Basic Difference

An FHA loan is backed by the Federal Housing Administration. That government guarantee means lenders can offer more flexible credit requirements and lower down payments.

A conventional loan isn't government-backed. It follows guidelines set by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. Generally, it requires stronger credit and a slightly higher down payment, but can save you money in the long run.

## When FHA Makes More Sense

FHA is often the better choice when:

- Your credit score is below 680
- You have limited savings and need the 3.5% down payment option
- You have higher debt relative to your income (FHA allows up to 57% DTI in some cases)
- You've had a recent credit event (bankruptcy, late payments)

## When Conventional Makes More Sense

Conventional is often the better choice when:

- Your credit score is 680 or higher
- You can put 10% or more down
- You want to avoid mortgage insurance (possible with 20%+ down)
- You're buying a higher-priced home above FHA limits

## The PMI Factor

Here's what most people miss: FHA loans have mortgage insurance for the life of the loan (in most cases). Conventional PMI drops off when you reach 20% equity.

That means a conventional loan with PMI may actually cost you less over time — even if the monthly payment looks similar upfront.

## The Bottom Line

There's no one-size-fits-all answer. I've put clients in FHA loans and I've put clients in conventional loans. The right choice depends on your credit, your savings, your goals, and how long you plan to stay in the home.
